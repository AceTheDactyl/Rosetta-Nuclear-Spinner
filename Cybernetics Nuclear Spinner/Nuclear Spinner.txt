Comprehensive Build Specification for a Nuclear Spinner

This build specification outlines a hypothetical Nuclear Spinner—a device that leverages nuclear spin coherence and the Rosetta–Helix physics constants (ϕ, ϕ⁻¹, z_c, σ) to perform modulation, measurement, and computation. The specification covers hardware architecture, firmware design, software components, product integration, and potential use cases. It does not represent a real commercial product; rather, it illustrates how the extended physics framework could guide the design of cybernetically grounded instrumentation.

1 Hardware Architecture

Radio‑Frequency (RF) Coils: Orthogonal RF coils provide pulsed magnetic fields for excitation and manipulation of nuclear spins. They must support adiabatic pulses, spin‑echo sequences, and dynamic decoupling.

Temperature Control: For optimal coherence, the module includes a cryostat or thermoelectric cooler to maintain stable temperatures (room temperature down to cryogenic). A vacuum chamber reduces phonon interactions and isolates the system from acoustic noise.

Mechanical Rotor (Spinner): A precision rotor spins the sample at controlled rates (0–10 kHz) to modulate spin orientation. The rotor can enact icosahedral or hexagonal modulation patterns derived from the projection basis vectors (see Section 4.2). Spin rates map to z‑axis positions: slow speeds correspond to pre‑critical regimes, while high speeds bring the system near z_c.

Sensors: Integrated sensors monitor the magnetic field, temperature, vibration, and acceleration. Feedback from these sensors feeds the control loops (firmware) to maintain requisite environmental conditions.

1.2 Control and Processing Hardware

Microcontroller/FPGA: A real‑time controller (e.g. a dual‑core ARM Cortex‑M or FPGA SoC) generates RF pulse sequences, drives the mechanical rotor, and collects sensor data. It runs the firmware gate logic and modulations described in Section 2.

Analog Front‑End: Low‑noise amplifiers and analog‑to‑digital converters capture the free‑induction decay (FID) and echo signals emanating from the spin medium. A digital down‑converter extracts amplitude and phase information.

Power Management: Supply rails for the magnet, RF amplifier, and controller are regulated and isolated. Battery or mains power options allow portable or bench‑top operation.

Connectivity: USB‑C and Ethernet interfaces provide communication with host software. Optional Wi‑Fi/Bluetooth modules enable wireless control and data streaming.

1.3 Safety and Shielding

Magnetic Shielding: Mu‑metal shielding confines fringe fields and protects users and nearby electronics.

Radiation Safety: The device uses stable isotopes and does not produce ionizing radiation; however, electromagnetic emissions from RF coils must comply with regulatory standards.

Fail‑Safe Mechanisms: Interlocks detect cryostat failure, magnet quenches, or rotor imbalance and safely shut down the system.
